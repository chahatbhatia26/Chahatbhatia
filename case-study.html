Professional product design work / Fintech onboarding

Reducing drop-offs in a high-friction mandate flow

New investors were reaching a required compliance step, then abandoning it because the flow felt opaque, high-risk, and hard to recover from after errors.

The redesign focused on making the step easier to understand, easier to trust, and easier to complete without weakening legal or technical requirements.

Role Lead Product Designer
Timeline 10 weeks
Team PM, researcher, 4 engineers, compliance
Impact Completion up, drop-offs down

Problem

A required step felt heavier than the value it unlocked.

Users needed to complete a mandate before they could start investing, but the experience felt legal-first, brittle under error states, and unclear about what would happen next. People had intent to continue, but the product was not giving them enough confidence to do it.

  • Progress felt unclear inside a high-trust financial step.
  • Error states appeared abrupt and hard to recover from.
  • Compliance language showed up before reassurance did.

Context

Why this mattered for both users and the business.

For users, confusion here felt expensive and irreversible. This was the point where trust could drop quickly, even if the rest of onboarding felt clear.

For the business, this was an activation bottleneck. Qualified users were getting through acquisition, but not crossing the last critical step needed to become active investors.

Approach

Short process, focused checkpoints.

01

Research

Reviewed session recordings, support tickets, and funnel exits to understand where confidence dropped before intent did.

02

Insights

Identified that orientation and recovery mattered more than adding more explanation everywhere.

03

Exploration

Tested clearer hierarchy, more visible progress, and calmer error states across the key decision points.

04

Validation

Reviewed flows with PM, engineering, and compliance to make sure the product improvements still held under real constraints.

Key decisions

Three changes that carried the outcome.

Reframed the step as guided confirmation.

What changed: the flow shifted from legal-first language to a clearer guided state with one primary action path.

Why: users needed orientation before they could trust what they were being asked to do.

Trade-off: more up-front explanation meant tighter prioritization of what not to show immediately.

Collapsed scattered notices into one persistent summary.

What changed: supporting information was consolidated into a stable summary instead of being repeated across screens.

Why: people needed the same context to stay visible as they progressed.

Trade-off: the summary component had to handle more states cleanly without becoming dense.

Designed explicit recovery for failed bank responses.

What changed: delayed or failed system states were rewritten as expected recovery paths instead of abrupt dead ends.

Why: technical uncertainty was being interpreted as product failure.

Trade-off: more recovery states increased design and QA scope, but reduced support confusion.

Design

Clarity, hierarchy, and recoverability made visible.

Impact

Directional outcomes, kept honest.

Completion Up

More users made it through the mandate step successfully.

Drop-offs Down

Abandonment reduced at the highest-friction point in onboarding.

Support load Lighter

Fewer users needed help understanding failures or next steps.

Learnings

What this sharpened in my product thinking.

  • Trust gaps are often hierarchy problems before they are content problems.
  • Recovery design matters most when technical systems are inconsistent.
  • Reducing friction does not always mean reducing information.
  • Strong product outcomes come from clarifying the decision, not polishing every surface.
